To separate the behaviour of one particular model from the structure of the task, we repeated the full main audit on a second open-weight model, \texttt{qwen3.5-9b} ($n=2{,}400$).
Table~\ref{tab:cross-model} sets the two models side by side.
The shape of every result transfers: income is again the dominant factor with a large effect; the refugee group is once more the sole background lifted clearly above the others (Figure~\ref{fig:qwen-heatmap-full}); and neither the gender main effect nor the gender$\times$background interaction reaches significance on Qwen (Cochran's $Q=0.70$, $p=0.95$).
The income-controlled view shows the same pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:owl-ablation} for owl-alpha; Figure~\ref{fig:qwen-ablation} for Qwen): almost no approvals at low income and a refugee premium that widens as income rises.
Restricting to the medium-income stratum, where affordability is roughly equal across applicants, leaves both the refugee premium and the absence of a gender gap intact (Figure~\ref{fig:qwen-heatmap-medium}).
\texttt{qwen3.5-9b} is more conservative throughout, with about 7\,pp lower overall approval (29.7\% vs.\ 36.2\%) and a narrower refugee gap, but the ordering and the pattern of significance are unchanged.

We also completed the full RQ4 mitigation sweep on Qwen (500 matched pairs per condition; Table~\ref{tab:qwen-rq4}).
Explicit fairness raises the overall Yes rate from 32.0\% to 39.0\% but does not remove background disparities; chain-of-thought (32.4\%) is close to baseline and again changes strictness more than fairness.
